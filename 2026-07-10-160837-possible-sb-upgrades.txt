‚óŹ Short answers first, then the paper.

  Does OneCovariance use triplet counts by default?

  No. In the reference config.ini, mix_term_do_mix_for is empty, and the comment
  says "LEAVE EMPTY ‚Ä¶ when the standard mix term should be considered" ‚ÄĒ i.e.
  the default is the analytic mixed term. Triplet counts are opt-in, and
  narrowly scoped: only for the mixed term, only for xipxip/xipxim/ximxim (not
  ő≥‚āú or w), and they require the actual galaxy catalog (RA/Dec, weights, zbin
  columns), patch-splitting of the sky, and hours of counting (which is why the
  counts are cached to FITS and reloadable).

  Can Spaceborne do it?

  Two paths. The lazy one works today: your oc_interface.py can already
  substitute OC's Gaussian term via G_code ‚ÄĒ configure OC's triplet-count mixed
  term in the generated .ini and you get it without writing a line of counting
  code. A native implementation would be a bigger deal: Spaceborne is
  map/config-driven and has no catalog input path at all, so you'd be adding a
  whole ingestion layer plus a counting engine (OC's is C++/ctypes). Not worth
  building natively ‚ÄĒ unless you go the map-based route below, which sidesteps
  catalogs entirely.

  The quadruplet-counts paper (Nagura, Terasawa, Terawaki & Takada)

  What it is: the exact expression for the sample-variance term of the
  pair-weighted őĺ estimator ‚ÄĒ Cov(őĺŠĶĘ,őĺ‚Īľ) = ő£ over quadruplets of w_a w_b w_c w_d
  √ó [őĺ(őł_ac)őĺ(őł_bd) + őĺ(őł_ad)őĺ(őł_bc)]/2, normalized by the pair counts, given a
  fiducial őĺ. This completes a neat hierarchy that answers your previous
  question exactly: pair counts fix the shot-noise term, triplet counts fix the 
  mixed term, quadruplet counts fix the sample-variance term ‚ÄĒ the one I said
  pair counts can't touch. So yes, it's the cutting-edge method for
  exact-geometry Gaussian real-space covariance, and notably it's not
  brute-force over galaxies: they coarse-grain onto HEALPix cells (Nside
  256‚Äď512) and evaluate a matrix expression over cell pairs, so it's really a

‚óŹ Because deconvolving the mixing matrix removes the bias of the estimator, not
  its variance. Those are two different things, and fsky lives in the second
  one.

  The mask does two separate damages:

  1. It distorts the mean: ‚ü®pseudo-Cl‚ü© = ő£ M_‚Ąď‚Ąď' C_‚Ąď'. This is what decoupling
  undoes ‚ÄĒ applying M‚ĀĽ¬Ļ gives you an estimator whose expectation value is the
  full-sky Cl. That's all "decoupled" promises.

  each ‚Ąď you measured roughly fsky¬∑(2‚Ąď+1) independent modes instead of (2‚Ąď+1).
  No linear operation on the data can recreate modes you never observed ‚ÄĒ M‚ĀĽ¬Ļ is
  just a reshuffling of the numbers you have.

  Full sky: N_modes = (2‚Ąď+1)őĒ‚Ąď. Cut sky: N_modes ‚Čą fsky¬∑(2‚Ąď+1)őĒ‚Ąď. Hence the Knox
  1/fsky ‚ÄĒ it's the price of the missing sky, and it's there whether or not
  you've debiased the mean. Same as ordinary statistics: if someone gives you
  only 30% of the samples, you can build a perfectly unbiased estimator of the
  mean, but its error bar is still ‚ąö(1/0.3) larger.
